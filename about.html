When I started my first lemonade stand

at 7 who knew I was forming a start-up. It started with cardboard scraps, a sharpie and a gallon of lemonade from the refrigerator. I became a neighborhood hit, but most importantly my lemonade stand helped me discover a few things about myself that would carry me throughout life (on repeat) that I pass on through my work.

I like to build things:

Learning was always a building block for me. I wanted to learn everything and anything. I enjoyed lego always as a kid, I hated dolls. Maybe I was destined to be an architect. I enjoyed all forms of art and surprised myself when I enrolled in business school. Maybe I could build an empire and become a CEO of some large corporation.

I surround myself around people that I could learn from:

Business school gave me a foundation, but my first “real” job is what set the tone. Right after graduate school, I landed a job as a coordinator for a television production company and I had the opportunity to travel cross-country with the film crew and learn about production. My U.S. journey then led me to Europe and Asia journeys. I met people from far away lands, was introduced to many cultures and learned the true meaning of “story”.

The people we filmed made me appreciate a true character, but although that was fiction, it opened my eyes to things I had never seen before. For 6 years, I traveled to as many places as I could, said “yes” to every new film opportunity that came my way, worked long hours and gave 200% to learning the in’s and out’s of production to eventually become a line producer.

I like a challenge:

Life’s unexpected twists happen and for me, it was a new family. This one wasn’t planned, but it was my blessing in disguise. After living out of my suitcase and racking up frequent flyer points from the production life, the opposite had hit when my daughter was born. Being a mom was hard. Being a single mom was even harder.

This little person was teaching me so much about life that I would never have expected. All those things that my parents said to me as a kid that I never understood suddenly became clear. I wanted my daughter to grow up in a safe world, have a voice and be the best version of herself she possibly could be. I knew traveling and going back to production life wasn’t an option for me anymore. I was now responsible for someone else’s life.

Enter the 2nd career at a design agency as their Marketing and Communications Director. As I told the journey of their story through the social media rise and watched technology expand and take over, it was exciting. But in recent years, I also saw the bad in how it was being used; Online bullying, violence, and a curated life that made others feel they were never good enough leading to self-doubt.

What kind of world would my daughter grow up in? Will she remember to have that human connection and experience things without a screen as I did growing into a young adult? Will she want to put her phone down and go outside and explore life?

I like to build things:

The technology isn’t going away anytime soon, so instead of pushing it aside, I want to design a better future. One that integrates both digital and real-life experiential moments to create those human connections through emotion. I want to evoke kindness back into the world and especially for the young adults out there. I refuse to believe that boundaries exist between disciplines and that there is still so much building to do.

Let’s tell better stories!
